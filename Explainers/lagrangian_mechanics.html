Lagrangian Mechanics

The fundamental premise of Lagrangian mechanics is that, among all the paths we could possibly conceive of, the physical paths that actually happen minimize a certain kind of energy. Unlike Newtonian mechanics, in which one writes down \(F = ma\) and solves the resulting differential equation, Lagrangian mecahnics considers the "first" and "last" state as fixed in time and space, and then the in-between is thing that minimizes energy.
To the state of a system, we can define a function \(\mathcal L\) of its position, velocity, and the current time. This function is called the Lagrangian. It has units of energy. Given an initial configuration \(a\) at time \(t_1\) and final configuration \(b\) at time \(t_2\), the action function is defined as \[ S = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \mathcal L dt = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \mathcal L( q(t), \dot{q}(t), t)dt \] then Hamilton's principle says the following:

Hamilton's Principle: Paths of motion minimize \(S\), subject to their initial values.

Many texts will provide a Lagrangian, and then say that Hamilton's principle applies to that particular Lagrangian. And with a totally arbitrary looking Lagrangian, we reproduce the laws of physics, and that is somehow a miracle! To gain intuition for these Lagrangians, let's actually work in reverse: figure out the ramifications of these eqtremal approaches, and then rectify them with known laws of physics.

Strictly Mathematical Properties of the Lagrangian

Let's begin with a mathematical discussion of Lagrangians and their associated action functional. The Lagrangian is a function of position and velocity \(\mathcal L(q, \dot{q}, t)\). While it's natural to think of \(q = (q_1 \ldots q_d)\) as Cartesian position and velocity, that's not necessary.

The Euler-Lagrange Equations

For simplicity, everything is going to be smooth. For a path \(q(t)\) of motion minimizing \(S\), it is of course necessary that a perturbation of \(q\) of the form \(q(t) + \beta \xi(t)\) is suboptimal for minimizing \(S\), so long as \(\xi\) has \(\xi(t_1) = \xi(t_2) = 0\). And since \(q\) is optimal, \[ \frac{\partial}{\partial \beta} S = 0. \] We make no assumption yet on the specific form of the Lagrangian, except that our action functional is defined from it. To be explicit mathematically, we will denote by \[ \frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial q} \] to be the gradient of \(\mathcal L\) with respect to its first argument, holding the second and third arguments fixed. We do the same for \(\dot{q}\) as well. Armed with this notation, \[ \begin{aligned} 0 & = \frac{\partial}{\partial \beta} S \\ & = \frac{\partial}{\partial \beta} \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \mathcal L(q + \beta \xi, \dot{q} + \beta \dot{\xi}, t)dt \\ & = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \frac{\partial}{\partial \beta} \mathcal L(q + \beta \xi, \dot{q} + \beta \dot{\xi}, t)dt & \text{ switching the order} \\ & = \sum_{n=1}^d \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial q_n} \xi_n(t) + \frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \dot{q_n}} \dot{\xi_n}(t)& \text{ by the Chain rule} \\ & = \sum_{n=1}^d \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial q_n} \xi_n(t) + \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \dot{q_n}} \frac{d}{dt} \xi(t) \\ & = \sum_{n=1}^d \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial q_n} \xi_n(t) - \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \xi(t) \frac{d}{dt} \frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \dot{q_n}} & \text{ Integrating by Parts, using } \xi(t_1) = \xi(t_2) = 0 \\ & = \sum_{n=1}^d \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \bigg(\frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial q_n} - \frac{d}{dt} \frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \dot{q_n}}\bigg) \xi(t)dt \end{aligned} \] and quite interestingly, this holds for arbitrary \(\xi\)! This can only be the case if the expression inside the integral is zero for all \(n\). This implies the Euler-Lagrange equation: \[ \frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial q} - \frac{d}{dt} \frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \dot{q}} = 0 \]

Noether's Theorem

In the Euler-Lagrange equations, it could be the case that the Lagrangian doesn't depend on a specific \(q_n\) of \(q = (q_1 \ldots q_d)\). A reasonable example are functions which depend on velocity, but not position (like kinetic energy, or momentum). This means that \(\frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial q}\) is zero, and thus that \[ \frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \dot{q_n}} = C_n \] for some constant \(C_n\). So to each "symmetry" (quantity on which the Lagrangian doesn't depend), there is a "conserved quantity" in time. This is the simplest statement of Noether's Theorem, although fancier generalizations do exist. The conserved quantity \(\frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \dot{q_n}}\) is called a generalized momentum.

"Energy" Conservation

The total derivative of the Lagrangian with respect to time is \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{d}{dt}\mathcal L & = \frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial q} \cdot \dot{q} + \frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \dot{q}} \cdot \ddot{q} + \frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial t} & \text{ by the chain rule} \\ & = \bigg(\frac{d}{dt} \frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \dot{q}}\bigg) \cdot \dot{q} + \frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \dot{q}} \cdot \ddot{q} + \frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial t} & \text{ by Euler-Lagrange} \\ & = \frac{d}{dt}\bigg( \frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \dot{q}} \cdot \dot{q} \bigg) + \frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial t} \end{aligned} \] implying \[ \frac{d}{dt}\bigg( \frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \dot{q}} \cdot \dot{q} - \mathcal L \bigg) = - \frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial t}. \] It is thus very interesting for when the Lagrangian does not depend on time (in the non-total sense that \(\frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial t} = 0\)), as this means \[ E \triangleq \frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \dot{q}} \cdot \dot{q} - \mathcal L\] is unchanging as time goes on. It is possible to unify this observation with the prior one, which I may save for a later version of this explainer.

Separable Lagrangians

Suppose further that the Lagrangian can be separated like \( \mathcal L = T(\dot{q}) - V(q).\) In this case, the Euler Lagrange equations imply that \[ \frac{d}{dt} \frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{q}} = -\frac{\partial V}{\partial q} \] This observation will be of greater interest later.

Lagrangians in Physics

Motivating Example: Conservative Forces

Consider once more the equation for a separable Lagrangian: \[ \frac{d}{dt} \frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{q}} = -\frac{\partial V}{\partial q} \] and compare this to the force due to a conservative force: \[ \frac{d}{dt} p = -\nabla V \] where \(V\) is the scalar potential. A sensible thing to do would be to construct \(\mathcal L = T - V \) so that \(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{q}} = p = m\dot{x} = \frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial x}\). And then we would just take \(V\) to be scalar potential energy. A solution to \[ \frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{q}} = m \dot{x} \] is for \(T = \frac{1}{2}m |\dot{v}|^2\). This is, of course, the kinetic energy. Therefore, in such a case, the choice of \(\mathcal L = T - V \) is a good Lagrangian. And in the presence of conservative forces, the principle of stationary action applies. Moreover, the four fundamental forces are all conservative ones, so this is a powerful statement.

Energy Conservation

We argued that for a Lagrangian that doesn't depend on time, the following quantity is conserved: \[ E \triangleq \frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \dot{q}} \cdot \dot{q} - \mathcal L\] Letting \(\mathcal L = T-V\) be our specified Lagrangian, this means that \[ E = \frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{q}} \cdot \dot{q} - (T-V) = m \dot{q} \cdot \dot{q} - T + V = 2T - T + V = T + V \] is always conserved. This is the conservation of energy. It is worth noting that the main reason this worked is because \(\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{q}} \cdot \dot{q} = 2T\).

What was Important?

Let's reflect on what we found, and what was needed:
  1. Assuming Newton's laws for a conservative force, we were able to prove that motions follow the principle of stationary action with the Lagrangian \(T-V\).
  2. Given the specific form of \(V = \frac{1}{2}m|\dot{q}|^2\), we could prove the conservation of energy.
In my mind, the question remains: which of these are necessary? Is there another order?

The Following are Equivalent

Consider the following laws of physics with a fixed notion of kinetic energy \(T = \frac{1}{2}m|\dot{q}|^2\): \[ \begin{aligned} A: & \hspace{0.25cm} m \ddot{q} = - \nabla V, \text{ for some } V. \\ B: & \hspace{0.25cm} \text{The principle of stationary action holds with } \mathcal L = T(\dot{q}) - V(q), \text{ for some } V. \\ C: & \hspace{0.25cm} T(\dot{q}) + V(q) \text{ is constant, for some } V. \end{aligned} \] We were able to deduce that \(A \implies B \implies C\). In particular, we showed that Newton's second law implies that \(V\) is the "right" function for conservation of energy.

Note that if \(C \implies A\) directly, then the three would all be logically equivalent. Indeed, \(C\) implies that \[ \frac{d}{dt} T(\dot{q}(t)) = - \frac{d}{dt} V(q(t)) \] \[ m\dot{q} \cdot \ddot{q} = - \nabla V \cdot \dot{q} \] and as \(\dot{q}\) is arbitrary in this expression, it must be that \(m \ddot{q} = - \nabla V \), which is \(A\). To summarize, this analysis has been interesting, but not coincidental! In particular, \(C \implies B \implies A\). In plain English, the conservation of energy implies Newton's second law, which is basically just the Euler-Lagrange equation for the Lagrangian \(\mathcal L = T-V\).

But What do We Gain?

One could say that law \(B\) is just a corollary of A and/or C and therefore brings nothing new to the table. At the same time, it greatly simplifies the problem-solving process. Energies are scalars, and calculus is easier than modeling.
