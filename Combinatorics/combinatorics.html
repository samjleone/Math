Binomial Fact 1.
The following holds for all \(0 \leq k \leq n\): \[ \binom{n}{k}\bigg(\frac{1}{4}\bigg)^{k} \bigg(\frac{3}{4}\bigg)^{n-k} = \sum_{m=k}^{n} \binom{n}{m} \binom{m}{k} \bigg(\frac{1}{2}\bigg)^{n} \bigg(\frac{1}{2}\bigg)^{m} \]
Proof:

Note first that, multiplying both sides of the above by \(4^n\), this is algebraically equivalent to, \[ 3^{n-k} \cdot \binom{n}{k} = \sum_{m=k}^{n} 2^{n-m} \binom{n}{m} \binom{m}{k} \] So it remains to show that the above is true. We shall prove this using a double couting argument. Suppose we are given \(n\) balls \(1,2...n\) and we want to color them using four colors: red, blue, yellow, and green. We also impose the constraint that exactly \(k\) of the balls are red. Let \(N_k\) be the number of unique ways to do this. We will prove the claim by showing both sides o the above are equal to \(N_k \).

First, observe \(N_k = 3^{n-k} \cdot \binom{n}{k}\). Why is this? Recall we want to color \(k\) balls red and the other balls any of blue, yellow, and green. There are \(\binom{n}{k}\) choices for the \(k\) red balls and, for each choice, \(3^{n-k}\) ways to color the others blue, yellow, and green. And thus, multiplying these, \(N_k = 3^{n-k} \cdot \binom{n}{k}\).

We are left to show that \( N_k = \sum_{m=k}^{n} 2^{n-m} \binom{n}{m} \binom{m}{k} \). Now, we count the number of ways to achieve our coloring like so. First, consider any \(k \leq m \leq n\). Now pick any \( m \) balls to color red or blue, of which there are \( \binom{n}{m} \) ways to do so. Now, of the \(m\) balls you have, pick \(k\) balls to color red and color the other \(m-k\) blue - there are \(\binom{m}{k}\) possibilities. Finally, color the other \(n-m\) balls yellow or green - there are \(2^{n-m}\) ways to do this. Thus, for a given \(m\), there are \(2^{n-m} \binom{n}{m} \binom{m}{k} \) ways to accomplish this coloring with. Summing over all possible \(m\)s, we at last have that there are \(N_K = \sum_{m=k}^{n} 2^{n-m} \binom{n}{m} \binom{m}{k}\) ways to color the balls as described. This concludes the proof.
Binomial Fact 2.
The following holds for all \( n \geq 0\), \[ \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{(-1)^k}{k}\binom{n}{k} = \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{1}{k} \]
Proof:

For this proof, we will combine calculus along with the binomial theorem. Consider the following integral: \[ I = \int_0^1 \frac{1}{y}((1-y)^n - 1)dy \] What is the value of \(I\)? Let us first expand the exponential term using the binomial formula: \[ I = \int_0^1 \frac{1}{y}\bigg(\sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k} (-y)^k \bigg - 1)=\int_0^1 \frac{1}{y} \sum_{k=1}^n \binom{n}{k} (-y)^k \] \[ = \int_0^1 \sum_{k=1}^n \binom{n}{k} (-1) (-y)^{k-1} = - \sum_{k=1}^n \binom{n}{k} \int_0^1 (-y)^{k-1} = \sum_{k=1}^n \binom{n}{k} \frac{1}{k} \bigg((-1)^{k} - (0)^k \bigg) \] So all in all, \[ I = \sum_{k=1}^n \binom{n}{k} \frac{(-1)^k}{k} \] On the other hand, we can calculate \(I\) through a change of variables. Note, letting \(u = 1-y, du = - dy\) \[ I = \int_0^1 \frac{1}{y}((1-y)^n - 1)dy = \int_0^1 \frac{1- u^n}{1-u}du \] This can be recognized as a finitee geometric series. Swapping the integral with the sum, evaluating, and reindexing, we find: \[ = \int_0^1 \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} u^k du = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \int_0^1 u^k du = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \frac{1}{k+1} = \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{1}{k} \] And thus, \[ \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{1}{k} = I = \sum_{k=1}^n \binom{n}{k} \frac{(-1)^k}{k} \] Which concludes the proof!
